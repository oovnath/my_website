Exploring Purulia: A Day of Discovery

Jaychondi, Banda Deul, Telkupi, Pruliya at spring

  • Raghunathpur, Puruliya
  • 17/03/2024

Without any prior planning, my wife and I, along with my newly married friend and his wife, embarked on a spontaneous journey to Jaychandi Hill in Purulia. Our adventure began from our hometown, leading us to Asansol Junction. From there, we boarded a train to ‘Jay Chandi Pahar’ station.

Upon arrival, we made our way to ‘Raghunathpur’, where we enjoyed some local delicacies, including ‘kachuri’, and refreshed ourselves with fruits like bananas and oranges. Our next destination was the ancient temple,“Banda Deul” . Given the lack of convenient public transportation options and considering we were a group of four, we decided to hire an auto-rickshaw. It turned out to be the perfect choice for us.

The journey was nothing short of magical. We were surrounded by vast expanses of green fields, punctuated by scattered small hills. In the distance, we could see a stretch of Palash forests, their red blossoms painting the horizon. It was as if Purulia had donned its spring attire. Even from the train, we could perceive the changing landscapes, a tapestry of nature’s artistry in motion.

The charming village of Banda, also known as Deulghera, is home to the 11th-century architectural wonder, Banda Deul. In 1872, archaeologist J.D. Beglar discovered this temple hidden under thick forest vegetation. After clearing the surrounding area, the temple began to draw interest. It is not clear whether it was a Hindu or a Jain temple.

Encircled by robust walls, the temple grounds are a verdant oasis, with lush green grass blanketing the area within. This area is meticulously preserved by the Archaeological Survey of India. Yet, it is beyond the temple’s perimeter that one finds the true essence of this area, The Palash Jungle. A natural spectacle, particularly in the vibrant spring season, the jungle is a mesmerizing sight with the fiery blooms of Palash trees igniting the landscape in a blaze of color.

We spent quite some time there, but we had another destination to visit, so we started our journey. But fate had a different plan charted out for us. Our journey took an unexpected turn when our driver guided us to the ancient “Moutorh Kali Temple”. We had no idea about this temple, but locals said that this was a thousand-year-old Kali temple. The temple is a significant place of worship for devotees and is renowned for ‘Poshu Boli’ Boli’ (although I do not support animal sacrifice in the name of religion). After visiting this temple, we went to Telkupi . According to Google, Tailakampi was a commercial hub in the 10th to 13th century.

Telkupi | A unique historical monument beside the river Damodar.

On that day, under the bright midday sun, we had to get out of the auto because there was no road to go to the Deul, so we stepped out. This place is so quiet. Beside the Damodar river, we followed a path through the crops. Then we walked through the tall grass to the river, where the boats were anchored without their sailors. The area around Deul was flat, sloping along the river and covered with greenery. Seeing Deul’s poor state by the river made me feel sad.

However, after spending some time there too, we headed back to Raghunathpur. After reaching there, we had lunch in a local hotel, with rice, dal, potato fry & mixed veg.

After lunch, we went to our last destination for that day, The Jaychondi Pahar Jaychondi Pahar. This is a popular tourist center and major attraction for rock climbing. However, it's an attraction to me and many other bengalis is because the famous film Satyajit Ray’s “Hirak Rajar Deshe” was shot here.

Semaphore Tower | The Calcutta-Chunar line was abandoned as a failed project in 1828.

We took a toto from Raghunathpur bazar to Jaychandi hill. There is a beautiful staircase leading up the mountain (Around 520 steps). Which starts just after the front door. Most of the stairs are not steep, so the climb is not difficult, and there is a place to sit at almost every corner of the road. On that way, we found many shops selling lemon juice, cucumber, etc.

Towards the end of reaching the top of the hill, I saw a historical ‘Semaphore Tower’ standing witness to many stories. As far as I know, this type was built during the British Raj for the optical telegram system. In those days (perhaps in the 1800s), it spread from Calcutta(Kolkata) to Chunar in Uttar Pradesh.

After reaching the top of the hill, there were two temples, a small Hanuman temple, and another Kali temple, but the 360 view from the top was amazing.

We stayed at the top for a while and then came down, had lemon juice on the way down. My friend and I bought toys made by local people, two toys were made of palm leaves. I thought then what simple things people used to be happy with, which is hard to imagine in today’s age of smart technology.

Kali Temple | Hanuman Temple | Palash Bon.

We reached Jay Chandi Pahar railway station from Jay Chandi hill at dusk, from there we reached Asansol , then towards our hometown. Along with this was the memory of a beautiful day spent at Purulia and Palash forest this spring.
